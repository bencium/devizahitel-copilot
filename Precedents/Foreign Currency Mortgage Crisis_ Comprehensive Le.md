# Foreign Currency Mortgage Crisis: Comprehensive Legal Research and Precedents for EU Consumer Protection Claims

## Executive Summary

Based on extensive research of European Court of Justice (ECJ) rulings, national legislation, and consumer protection precedents from 2005-2025, **foreign currency mortgage borrowers have strong grounds for compensation claims** based on inadequate disclosure of currency fluctuation risks. The most recent ECJ rulings, particularly the landmark **Szcześniak v Bank M. SA (C-520/21)** decision of June 2023, have established that banks are **not entitled to interest on invalidated contracts** and consumers can claim **full restitution** including compensation beyond monthly payments.[^1][^2][^3]

## Key Legal Precedents Supporting Your Claim

### ECJ Case C-520/21: Arkadiusz Szcześniak v Bank M. SA (June 15, 2023)

This **most significant ruling for Polish borrowers** established that:

- Banks **cannot charge interest** on Swiss franc mortgages declared invalid due to unfair terms[^2][^1]
- Consumers can seek **compensation beyond reimbursement** of monthly installments paid[^4]
- Banks are **prohibited from claiming similar compensation** against consumers[^4]
- **No limitation period** applies for consumers seeking restitution on unfair contract terms[^5]


### ECJ Case C-186/16: Andriciuc v Banca Românească SA (September 20, 2017)

The foundational ruling establishing that **financial institutions must provide adequate information** about currency risks, including:

- **Impact on installments** of severe depreciation of national currency[^6][^7][^8]
- **Possible variations** in exchange rates and inherent risks[^7][^6]
- Information must enable consumers to **estimate significant economic consequences**[^8][^6]
- **Plain intelligible language** requirement extends beyond grammatical clarity to actual economic understanding[^9][^7]


### ECJ Case C-705/21: MJ v AxFina Hungary Zrt. (April 27, 2023)

Ruled that when foreign currency contracts are invalid due to unfair terms:

- **Full restitution** must restore consumers to pre-contractual position[^10][^11]
- Contracts placing **exchange rate risk entirely on consumers** without adequate disclosure are unfair[^12][^13]
- **Retroactive cancellation** is required when contracts cannot exist without unfair terms[^11][^13]


## Hungarian Legal Framework and Recent Developments

### 2025 ECJ Breakthrough Ruling (C-630/23)

The **most recent ECJ ruling from April 30, 2025** regarding Hungarian foreign currency leasing contracts established:

- **Complete contract invalidation** when unfair currency terms cannot be separated[^13]
- **Full restitution obligations** for all payments made by consumers[^10][^11]
- This ruling is expected to **reignite litigation** on foreign currency contracts in Hungary[^11][^10]


### Hungarian Government Response (2014-2015)

Hungary implemented **mandatory conversion** of foreign currency mortgages:

- **Act XXXVIII of 2014**: Required banks to convert CHF mortgages to Hungarian Forints[^14][^15][^16]
- **Conversion rate**: Used market rates at time of conversion, not original favorable rates[^16][^17]
- **Limited scope**: Only addressed future currency risk, **no compensation for past losses**[^18][^17][^16]


### Consumer Protection Organizations

**Banki Károsultak Fogyasztóvédelmi Szövetsége** (Consumer Protection Association for Bank Victims) provides:

- **Free consultation** for foreign currency mortgage victims
- **Legal assistance** within lawful frameworks
- **MNB verification**: Hungarian National Bank confirmed their legal operation


## Legal Grounds for Your Compensation Claim

### 1. Inadequate Risk Disclosure

Based on **Andriciuc precedent**, banks violated EU Directive 93/13/EEC by failing to:

- Explain **currency fluctuation mechanisms** in plain language[^6][^7][^8]
- Provide **quantitative scenarios** showing payment increases[^3][^6]
- Disclose **disproportionate risk allocation** to consumers[^19][^5]


### 2. Unfair Contract Terms Under Directive 93/13/EEC

Your mortgage likely contained **unfair terms** including:

- **Currency conversion clauses** using different buy/sell rates[^20][^21][^6]
- **Unilateral variation rights** for banks to adjust terms[^22][^23]
- **Exchange rate risk transfer** without adequate compensation[^5][^19]


### 3. Recent ECJ Clarifications on Restitution

**Szcześniak ruling** confirms your right to:

- **Full refund** of all payments made during mortgage term[^1][^2][^3]
- **No interest obligations** to banks on invalidated contracts[^2][^4][^1]
- **Additional compensation** for opportunity costs and damages[^4][^1][^2]
